The Problem

Most SaaS businesses know their churn rate. Few know where the revenue is actually escaping.

A monthly churn rate of 3% sounds manageable. But 3% on a growing base means the absolute MRR lost each month is accelerating — and if that loss is concentrated in a specific segment, contract type, or time of year, a single number hides the story entirely.

The standard approach is a dashboard showing revenue going up. What's missing is the decomposition — how much came from new customers, how much from upgrades, how much was lost to cancellations, and how much was quietly lost to downgrades.

The business question this project answers: "Where exactly is revenue leaking — and how urgently does each leak need to be fixed?" Not just a rate, but a prioritised diagnosis.

Key Findings

What the data revealed about this SaaS business in 2024.

01

Explosive growth masking a deteriorating leak

MRR grew 672% in 2024 — from £1.26M in January to £9.74M in December. But the Leak Score spiked to 8.7% in December, with 117 churn events — nearly 3× November's 40. The business is growing fast and leaking faster at year-end.

02

The December crisis is structural, not random

December saw £574,643 in churned MRR — more than the combined total of the previous five months. Annual contracts cluster non-renewals at year-end. The pattern is predictable, which means it is preventable.

03

The upgrade-then-churn paradox

123 customers (20.5% of all churn events) upgraded to a higher tier and still churned. This signals a product-market fit ceiling at the premium tier — customers paid more, expected more, and were disappointed.

04

Pricing vs features — a value perception split

Exit feedback: 35.6% "too expensive", 34.3% "missing features", 30.1% "switched to competitor". This is not a price problem — it is a value delivery problem. Features before price cuts is the higher-leverage fix.

05

24.7% of churners left without any feedback

148 customers cancelled without completing an exit survey. Silent churners are the most frustrated — they have given up on being heard. Improving exit survey completion is the lowest-effort, highest-visibility intervention.

06

Win-back is already working

61 customers (10.2% of churn events) reactivated after churning. At an average MRR of £1,879, a structured win-back programme has an estimated recovery potential of over £100,000 annually.

Chart Design

The waterfall chart tells the story in under 5 seconds.

The chart uses a stacked column structure with 6 series and an invisible connector technique — Excel cannot natively float negative bars below a gross peak, so Churned and Contraction are entered as positive values that stack inside the bar, visually consuming the top of the gross peak.

Grey = what was already there. Green/teal = what came in. Red/amber = what leaked out. The Leak Score line on the secondary axis (0–12%) shows how bad the leak was each month — peaking dramatically in December.

What's Next

Knowing what's missing is part of the analysis.

Cohort Analysis

Track signup cohorts through their full lifecycle for LTV modelling — converting the snapshot waterfall into a longitudinal retention curve.

Segment-Level Leak Scores

Break down Leak Scores by plan tier to find which segment drives December's spike — aggregate metrics obscure the concentration risk.

Live Connection

Stripe or ChartMogul API to replace the monthly manual CSV refresh — eliminating the human step between close and insight.

Predictive Leak Score

Forward-looking alert using monthly trend, not a retrospective report — flagging deterioration before it hits the P&L.
